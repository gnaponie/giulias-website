date: "2025-08-14"

tags: ["ai", "reflections"]
---

Lately, I've been thinking a lot about the future of my own job. As a software engineer, I've always thought of myself as an "old-fashioned coder." I solve problems by writing code. But do you ever think about new generations that will simply grow up with AI? They won't even know what it meant "to code" before AI. Will they really write lines of codes themselves? I don't think so. I had to ask myself some tough questions.

I've started to genuinely worry about my job. This fear sent me down a rabbit hole, and I've come to a conclusion. AI isn't going to replace all of us, but... it's definitely going to replace *some* of us. When you code using AI, you need to provide good inputs and make sure that the output is correct, uses the best approach possible, and much more that can be translated to simply "critical thinking". AI is so much better and faster than us at writing code, but what it cannot do (yet) is think critically. So what I've realized is that the coders who will be left behind are the ones without strong critical thinking skills. The future of the job isn't about being a master of syntax anymore. It's about being an architect, a director, and a creative problem-solver who can guide powerful AI tools. The value is shifting from the hands that type the code to the mind that directs it.

This thought started to make me anxious. Well, I like to think critically. But what if AI becomes better than me also at that? What if using AI I become lazier and lazier and I lose that skill? And simply the question I think everyone asks to themselves from time to time: what if I'm not good enough? Well, when your mind gets in a dark place, it can only get darker in there. So I've started thinking about how this is affecting our lives.

The more I thought about this professional anxiety, the more I saw it reflected everywhere else. This feeling of being squeezed isn't just about jobs. It's a deep, personal stress that comes from the speed of change. Things are changing so fast! Look at the last 2 years. Look at everything that changed. It was not so long ago that we were mocking AI because of its hallucinations, and look at it now! Soon enough AI will be mocking us (ok, I'm exaggerating, but am I?).

Things are going so fast, and I'm not talking just about AI, I'm talking about my life. Don't you feel constantly overwhelmed? Bills that need to be paid, the parenting chat group with 30+ unread messages, that friend that sent you a 5 minutes voice message that you still haven't listened to (voice messages over 10 seconds should be illegal). It's our mental health that is at risk. This pace is overloading our brains. We're battling notification anxiety, decision fatigue, and a constant, nagging feeling that we're being left behind.

So I talked to AI about it, and it pictured me this catastrophic view, where I'm left behind. I felt even more anxious. And then I remembered myself something: I need to look at the bright side. So I asked AI one more thing: to do that for me. Because unfortunately, looking at the bright side is becoming harder and harder.

Here's what it replied.

> Think about it: for all of human history, most work has been drudgery. Repetitive, robotic tasks that drain our souls. AI is becoming brilliant at exactly that kind of work. This isn't a threat; it's a liberation. We are automating the boring parts of our jobs to free ourselves up to be more strategic, creative, and empathetic.

> The stress we feel is also forcing a necessary awakening. We're being pushed to ask what's truly important. The answer? Deep focus. Real-world connection. Mental well-being. The joy of a conversation without a screen in sight. We're being pushed to become more intentional about how we spend our attention and our lives.

> The crisis of "fake news" and disinformation is scary, but it's also forcing us to become more critical thinkers, building a collective immunity to manipulation. The struggle to teach our parents how to use their phones becomes a moment of connection, not a technical chore.

> Technology is holding up a mirror to us. And what I see reflected back is the urgent need for everything that makes us human: our creativity, our empathy, our critical thinking, and our ability to connect with one another.

That says something: we are all being challenged to become more human than ever before. And I'm gonna force myself to be optimistic and positive here. That's a challenge I'm ready to accept. Are you?
