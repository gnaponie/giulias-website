---
title: "Am I the broken rung?"
date: "2021-12-03"
excerpt: "Reflections on bias, confidence, and the invisible barriers women face in tech."
tags: ["women in tech", "diversity"]
---

I am a python programmer, a software engineer and a woman. I am a woman in tech. For the first part of my career I never really thought that it was "a thing". I just liked computer science. I didn't really care about it when in high school I chose the programming path and it was just me and other 3 girls in a class of 20 people. I didn't care when I graduated in Computer Science and women were 10% of the class. It didn't even matter in the beginning of my first tech job. It started to matter when those small (and sometimes not-that-small) things happen to, I think, every woman in IT: harassment, impostor syndrome and those questions "is this person questioning my abilities because I am a woman?". I guess those questions arrive for everyone at some point.

I wasn't happy in my first job, obsolete technologies, obsolete people, no meritocracy. But somehow I never thought that I was good enough to have something more.

I went to a python conference and a girl had a talk about how women don't take chances. How they don't apply to a job position if they don't match at least 90% of the job description, and how men would apply even if they match 40%. She talked about how women are trained, for all their lives, to be princesses, not to climb that tree, not to cover your pants with ground, simply to behave. And I've never been that person. I've never been what society wanted me to be. And then it hit me. It did matter that I was a woman in tech. It matters a lot.

Friends tell me I'm a strong person, even though I don't believe it. So I found strength, and with my poor English (I hope it has improved since then!) and what I thought were my poor abilities, I've started applying for open job positions. At first I applied to whatever had "python" as a prerequisite, almost not even reading the job description. And then I saw interviews going well. I started to think that I might not even be that bad. And then, I saw the perfect position in the perfect company. My impostor syndrome told me for months (years?) that it was impossible that somehow I've got the job. But I did it. And now, 4 years later, I'm a Senior Software Engineer at a great company.

In my company people care a lot about people, we care about diversity, inclusion and women. And that's when I started to do my part: speak at conferences (even though I was afraid), contribute to pyladies, have talks about women in tech, read about the topic, talk about the topic, all sorts of things. And I've first learned about the glass ceiling and secondly about the broken rung. The glass ceiling is a metaphor of an invisible barrier that prevents women from improving in their career. It's like there's something stopping us from moving forward. The broken rung is another metaphor that actually points out that the real problem is usually the step above you, such as your manager. You might have a community helping you, but if your manager is not helping you in your career, it's very difficult for you to emerge. I've been lucky enough to have a good community, be part of a great company, but also not to have a broken rung ahead of me. My last manager (a woman too) helped me get my promotion, she shared my success with higher management, and so on.

I'm at a point in my career where I am part of the interview process for new candidates. One day I had to screen many interns' CVs, and I had to choose 7 of them. There were a lot of very good candidates, and it was really hard to choose only 7 of them. I was pleasantly surprised to notice that many women applied. At the end of my decision… I've selected not even one woman for the interview! How was that possible? So many people applied, and not a simple woman? I gave myself 2 reasons for that:

1. I was reading a book about confidence, *The Confidence Code* (I didn't like it that much, but there were some nice and true thoughts in there — maybe a topic for another post) and it mentioned how women tend to be less confident and this might be reflected also in their CV. Maybe they're awesome at python, but they just write "beginner". And the opposite might be for some men.
2. It didn't matter all my experience as a woman in tech, I was biased myself! Did I automatically think that women are less good just because they are women (even though I know that's not true)?

So here's what I did: I took the less convincing CV of the 7 I chose, and I took the best CV among the women that applied. And do you want to know how the story ends? She rocked. She was great, she had the most complete python knowledge of all the candidates. Not a single wrong answer. But she was the least confident one. When I asked the questions she was almost afraid. Like I was asking impossible stuff. And she nailed it. And the worst thing? We didn't choose her. Other candidates did better in other topics and they were more confident about them. She looked a less strong candidate, just because of her confidence. So confidence does matter, not just competence.

I couldn't help myself thinking: am I the broken rung? Did I just prevent that woman from having a great career in a great company? Probably not, she's smart, and she'll succeed in life. But maybe, I could have fought more for her. And convince my colleagues that she was the best, after all.

So to summarize, I am biased, unfortunately. I'm a human myself and I make mistakes. But I try to recognize them, and fix them. And what about you? Are you the broken rung?
